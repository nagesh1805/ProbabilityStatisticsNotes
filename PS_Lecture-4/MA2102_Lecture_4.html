Probability is a tool for expressing degree of confidence or doubt about some proposition in the presence of incomplete information.By convention, probabilities are calibrated on a scale of 0 to 1. Probability statements that we make can be based on our past experience or subjective personal judgments, in either case they obey a common set of rules(principles), which can be used to treat probabilities in a mathematical framework.

We are going to think of probability theory as mathematical model of chance. Here the idea is to start with few basic principles(axioms) of chance that are sufficiently simple enough so that one feel them readily (no one suppose to question them). Once these principles(axioms) are accepted then we deduce the mathematical theory from these axioms that guide us in more complicated situations.

Random experiment:
An experiment in which,

  • the outcome of purticular performance cannot be predicted in advance.
  • the collection all possible outcomes can be described prior to its performance.
  • the experiment can be repeated under identical conditions.

Trial:A particular performance of a random experiment is called a trial.

Probability model that we are going talk with respect to random experiment has three main components, they are,
Sample space: Collection of all possible outcomes of random experiment is called Sample space, which is usually denoted by $\Omega$ or $S$.
Event space: Collection of all events of a random experiment is called Event space, which is usually denoted by $\mathscr{F}$ or $\mathscr{B}$.
An event is a property that can be observed either to hold or not to hold after experiment is done. Mathematically an event is subset of sample space. (i.e) if $E$ is an event then $E\subset \Omega$
Probability measure: For each event $E$ in $\mathscr{F}$(Event space) we associate a number chosen between 0 and 1 and called the probability of an event $E$ and it is denoted by $P(E)$.This number measures the likelihood of the event E to occur. The more likely the event is, the closer to 1 this number is.

Let us consider a random experiment of tossing a coin until we get head, we have sample space, $\Omega=\{H,TH,TTH,TTTH,TTTTH,..... \}$
now let us have a look at various events of interest with respect to this experiment.
Let $E_{i}$ be the event of getting head on (2i-1)th trial,for i=1,2,3,...
i.e $E_{i}=\{\underbrace{TTT...TH}_\text{2i-1 times}\}$ for i=1,2,3...

Let $E=\bigcup_{i=1}^{\infty}E_{i} \leftarrow$ requiring odd number of trials to see head
then $E^{c}$ denote the event of requiring even number of trials too see head i.e $E=\{H,TTH,TTTTH,...\underbrace{TTT...TH}_\text{odd times}... \}$
and $E^{c}=\{TH,TTTH,TTTTTH,...\underbrace{TTT...TH}_\text{even times}... \}$
As we can see from above example if $E_{1},E_{2},E_{3},...E_{n},...$ are events of interest then $\bigcup_{i=1}^{\infty}E_{i}$ also an event of interest, and if $E$ is an event of interest then $E^{c}$ also an event of interest.
Question: What should be our event space?
As we saw, we are interested in assigning probabilities to events, compliment of events and union of events and so on. Hence our event space should be rich enough to include all these combinations of events, that is event space should be stable with these union(also countable union) and complementation operations. Such a collection of events is called $\sigma$-Field of subsets of $\Omega$

Definition($\sigma$-Field): Let $\mathscr{F}$be a collection of subsets of $\Omega$. We say $\mathscr{F}$ is $\sigma$-Field on $\Omega$ if,
i). $\emptyset \in \mathscr{F}$ (i.e $\mathscr{F}$ is not empty)
ii). If $E\in \mathscr{F}$ then $E^{c}\in \mathscr{F}$ (i.e $\mathscr{F}$ is closed under complementation)
iii). If $E_{1},E_{2},E_{3},...E_{n},... \in \mathscr{F}$ then $\bigcup_{i=1}^{\infty}E_{i} \in \mathscr{F}$(i.e i.e $\mathscr{F}$ is closed under countable unions )

Remark 1: $\Omega$ always in $\mathscr{F}$
proof: $\emptyset \in \mathscr{F}$ by (i) $\Rightarrow \emptyset ^{c}=\Omega \in \mathscr{F}$ by (ii)
Remark 2: $\mathscr{F}$ is also closed under countable intersections. ( i.e If $E_{1},E_{2},E_{3},....$ in $\mathscr{F}$ then $\bigcap_{i=1}^{\infty}E_{i}$ also in $\mathscr{F}$ )
proof: If $E_{i}\in \mathscr{F} \Rightarrow E_{i}^{c}\in \mathscr{F}$ by (ii) for i=1,2,3,...
$\Rightarrow \bigcup_{i=1}^{\infty}E_{i}^c \in \mathscr{F}$ ( by (iii) ), and $\bigcup_{i=1}^{\infty}E_{i}^{c}= (\bigcap_{i=1}^{\infty}E_{i})^{c}$ ($\because$ De Morgan's law)
so $(\bigcap_{i=1}^{\infty}E_{i})^{c} \in \mathscr{F}$ $\Rightarrow ((\bigcap_{i=1}^{\infty}E_{i})^{c})^{c}=\bigcap_{i=1}^{\infty}E_{i} \in \mathscr{F}$ ( by (ii) & $(A^{c})^{c}=A$)
$\therefore \mathscr{F}$ is closed under countable intersections.

Remark 3: If $\mathscr{F}_1,\mathscr{F}_2$ are two $\sigma$-Fields on $\Omega$ then $\mathscr{F}_1\cap\mathscr{F}_2$ also $\sigma$-Fields on $\Omega$
proof: Here $\mathscr{F}_1,\mathscr{F}_2$ both are $\sigma$-Fields on $\Omega$, so $\emptyset \in \mathscr{F}_1$ and $\emptyset \in \mathscr{F}_2$ ($\because$ both $\mathscr{F}_1, \mathscr{F}_2$ are $\sigma$-Fields, so by (i)) )
If $E\in \mathscr{F}_1\cap\mathscr{F}_2$ then $E\in \mathscr{F}_1$ and $E\in \mathscr{F}_2$ that imply $E^c\in \mathscr{F}_1$ and $E^c\in \mathscr{F}_2$ ($\because$ both $\mathscr{F}_1, \mathscr{F}_2$ are $\sigma$-Fields, so by (ii)) )
$\therefore$ $E^c\in \mathscr{F}_1\cap\mathscr{F}_2$
If $E_1,E_2,E_3,...\in \mathscr{F}_1\cap\mathscr{F}_2$ that imply $E_1,E_2,E_3,...\in \mathscr{F}_1$ and $E_1,E_2,E_3,...\in \mathscr{F}_2$
then $\cup_{i=1}^{\infty}E_i\in \mathscr{F}_i$ for $i=1,2$ ($\because$ both $\mathscr{F}_1, \mathscr{F}_2$ are $\sigma$-Fields, so by (iii)) )

Remark 4: $\mathscr{F}$ is also closed under finite unions. ( i.e If $E_{1},E_{2},E_{3},....E_n$ in $\mathscr{F}$ then $\bigcup_{i=1}^{n}E_{i}$ also in $\mathscr{F}$ )
proof: Take $E_{n+i}=\emptyset \forall i\ge1$
then $E_1,E_2,...E_n,E_{n+1},...\in \mathscr{F} \Rightarrow \bigcup_{i=1}^{\infty}E_i \in \mathscr{F}$ by (iii)
but $\bigcup_{i=1}^{n}E_i=\bigcup_{i=1}^{\infty}E_i$ ($\because$ $E_{n+i}=\emptyset \forall i\ge1$ ) $\therefore~ \bigcup_{i=1}^{n}E_i \in \mathscr{F}$

Remark 5: $\mathscr{F}$ is also closed under finite intersections. ( i.e If $E_{1},E_{2},E_{3},....E_n$ in $\mathscr{F}$ then $\bigcap_{i=1}^{n}E_{i}$ also in $\mathscr{F}$ )

Examples:

  1. $\mathscr{F}_{o}=\{ \emptyset ,\Omega\}$ is a $\sigma$-Field on $\Omega$ (trivial $\sigma$-Field)
  2. $\mathscr{F}_{1}=\mathscr{P}(\Omega)$ is a $\sigma$-Field on $\Omega$ (trivial $\sigma$-Field) where $\mathscr{P}(\Omega)$ denote the power set of $\Omega$
  3. For any $E\subset \Omega$, $\mathscr{F}_{2}=\{\emptyset ,E,E^{c},\Omega \}$ is a $\sigma$-Field on $\Omega$
  4. $\mathscr{F}_{3}=\{(a,b]:a,b\in \mathbb{R},a<b\}$ is not a $\sigma$-Field on $\mathbb{R}$
  5. Let $\Omega$ is infinite, and $\mathscr{F}_{4}=\{A:A\subset \Omega,A$is finite$\}$ is not a $\sigma$-Field on $\Omega$

If some collection of subsets of $\Omega$ that we are interested is not happened to be $\sigma$-Field then we look for smallest $\sigma$-Field that contains the collection that we are interested in.

Definition: If $\mathscr{C}\subset \mathscr{P}(\Omega)$, then the $\sigma$-Field generated by $\mathscr{C}$, and written as $\sigma(\mathscr{C})$ is the smallest $\sigma$-Field containing $\mathscr{C}$
i.e $~\sigma(\mathscr{C})=\bigcap_{\underbrace{\mathscr{F}}_\text{$\sigma$ -Field}\supset \mathscr{C}} \mathscr{F}$

If $\Omega=\mathbb{R}$ and $\mathscr{I}=\{(a,b]:a,b\in \mathbb{R},a<b\}$($\mathscr{I}$ is not $\sigma$-Field), then we write $\mathscr{B}=\sigma(\mathscr{I})$ is called the Borel $\sigma$-Field (this $\mathscr{B}$ important one for us).That is Borel $\sigma$-Field is the smallest $\sigma$-Field on $\mathbb{R}$ that contains half open intervals(intervals of the form (a,b]). And elements of Borel $\sigma$-Field $\mathscr{B}$ are called the Borel sets of $\mathbb{R}$

Remarks: $\mathscr{B}$ contains,

  • All singletons, ($\because \{x\}=\bigcap_{n=1}^{\infty}(x-\frac{1}{n} ,x]$ )
  • All closed intervals, ($\because [x,y]=(x,y]\cup \{x\}$ )
  • All open intervals. ($\because (x,y)=(x,y]\cap \{y\}^{c}$ )
  • All semi-open intervals of the form $[x, y)$. ($\because [x,y)=(x,y)\cup \{x\}$ )
  • All semi-infinite intervals of the form $(x,\infty)$ ($\because (x,\infty)=\cup_{n=1}^{\infty}(x, x+n]$)
  • All semi-infinite intervals of the form $[x,\infty)$ ($\because [x,\infty)=(x, \infty)\cup \{x\}$)
  • All semi-infinite intervals of the form $(-\infty,x)$ ($\because (-\infty,x)=[x, \infty)^{c}$)
  • All semi-infinite intervals of the form $(-\infty,x]$ ($\because (-\infty,x]=(-\infty,x)\cup \{x\}$)
  • And all finite sets, and countable subsets of $\mathbb{R}$ (how!)

so Borel $\sigma$-Field contains all types of intervals and any subset of $\mathbb{R}$ that can be expressed in terms of these intervals through union(or countable union), intersection (or countable intersection) and complement operations.

Note: $\mathscr{B}\subsetneq \mathscr{P}(\mathbb{R})$, that is not every subset of $\mathbb{R}$ is Borel set of $\mathbb{R}$(it is hard to construct one non-Borel set but they do exists). But good news is all subsets of $\mathbb{R}$ that we can ever think of or of any practical interest are included in Borel $\sigma$-Field $\mathscr{B}.$

For a random experiment under consideration, we have sample space $\Omega$, and certain subsets of $\Omega$ which are events(we may not be interested in considering all subsets of $\Omega$ as events). If $\mathscr{F}$ is $\sigma$-Field of events in $\Omega$, then $(\Omega,\mathscr{F})$ is called a measurable space and elements of $\mathscr{F}$ are called measurable sets.

If we want to develop a theory of probability as a mathematical model of chance, we need a perfect definition for the concept of probability to start with. The definition that are going to use, consist of three axioms, which are motivated by three intuitive properties of relative frequency.

Relative frequency approach:.
Suppose we repeat the experiment independently n times, under identical conditions and $E$ be the event of interest,and
$f_{n}(E)$:= number of times the event $E$ happened in n trials
In the relative frequency method of assigning probabilities, probability of event $E$ is given by
$P(E):= \lim_{n \to \infty}\frac{f_{n}(E)}{n}$

If we toss a fair coin for a large number of times then the relative frequency of head occurring seems to stabilize at 0.5 (see plot below)

This relative frequency approach intuitively make sense and it is very practical approach, but to be able to use this definition to compute probability, one has to perform experiment a large number of times, which may not be feasible and this relative frequency approach is not suitable for developing mathematical theory of probability. So we are not going to start with this definition.

Drawbacks of Relative frequency approach:

  • Actual observation of the experiment may not be possible
  • Experiment may be too costly to perform,then cannot afford to repeat it large number of times.
  • we don't know if limit always exists (actually we will show later in this course (using Law of Large numbers) that this limit indeed exist in some probability sense)
